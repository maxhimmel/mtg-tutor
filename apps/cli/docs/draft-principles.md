# MTG Limited: Draft & Deckbuilding Principles

Set-agnostic, evergreen principles for drafting and building 40-card Limited decks.
Distilled from the credible strategy sources listed at the end. Intended as a grounding / fact-check reference for reasoning about card evaluation and deck construction — independent of any specific set.

## Reading Signals & Staying Open

- Do not commit to a color or archetype at the start of the draft; your first pick does not need to end up in your final deck.
- Take the best card in the pack for roughly your first five picks, then evaluate and commit to a two-color pair.
- A signal is a passed card that indicates a color is open; you cannot read signals without a foundation of card evaluation.
- Read signals by noticing what is _absent_ — when strong cards are missing from a pack, upstream drafters are taking that color.
- A premium/bomb-quality card that shows up much later than expected (e.g. a stone-cold bomb at pick 4) means the players ahead of you are not in that color — it is open.
- Take a strong late-passed card even if it conflicts with your current plan; the late pick itself is the signal that the lane is open.
- Abandon an early-picked color if strong cards in it stop coming; even first picks are expendable, and top players commonly drop at least one of their first three picks.
- No single signal should be weighted heavily — a signal is a probabilistic hint, never proof, and some packs inevitably send misleading messages.
- Signals work because adjacent drafters implicitly cooperate: cards passed between neighbors let both end up with stronger decks by moving into open colors.
- Waiting to accumulate "enough" signals before committing is a trap — by the time signals give a confident read, the window to switch colors has usually already closed.
- Have your deck plan mostly settled by around the middle of pack 2, while still reassessing the strength of your second color.
- Almost never switch colors in pack 2 unless you open or are passed an exceptional rare.
- Effective signal reading depends on format knowledge: themes, the top commons/uncommons, and archetype "signpost" cards that pull you in a direction.
- One way to stay open is to evaluate each pick in a vacuum on absolute power, so you correctly identify the strongest cards regardless of what you already have.
- An alternative is to navigate pack 1 so you end up mostly in a single color, leaving you free to take any bomb opened at the start of pack 2.
- Cards you have already drafted should influence later picks and serve as tiebreakers between otherwise-equal options.

## Card Evaluation Fundamentals

- BREAD is the classic pick-priority framework: **B**ombs, **R**emoval, **E**vasion, **A**ggro (aggressive/advantage cards), **D**uds — ranked in descending draft priority.
- A bomb is a high-power card that, with reasonable certainty, wins the game on its own; an unanswered bomb usually wins, which is why bombs are the top priority.
- Removal is premium because you must be able to eliminate opposing threats and disrupt the opponent's plan; more removal generally improves a deck.
- Evasion (flying, menace, trample, "can't be blocked") is valued because it lets creatures bypass blockers and deal damage directly to the player.
- Aggressive/cheap cards are potent specifically because Limited has fewer removal options than Constructed, so fast early damage is hard to answer.
- Duds are unplayable cards (too expensive or too little impact) that belong in the sideboard.
- Prioritize cards that affect the board state — creatures, removal, combat tricks — over effects like raw card draw that don't directly impact the physical board.
- Modern critique of BREAD: it can overvalue removal and evasion and undervalue board presence, since most Limited games are won on the board. One reframing is CABS (Cards Affecting Board State), roughly creatures > removal > combat tricks.
- Another critique: high-quality common creatures in modern sets often outclass mediocre removal, so don't blindly take removal over a great creature.
- Use a tier system rather than absolute rankings: when two cards are of comparable power, pick the one that best fits your developing deck.
- Favor consistency over ceiling — cards that are reliably good beat cards that are sometimes great but sometimes dead.

## Archetypes — What Each Is Trying To Do

- **Aggro** aims to end the game as fast as possible with a critical mass of cheap creatures; it runs a low curve, a lower land count (~16), and minimal expensive cards or removal.
- **Control** uses cheap interaction and removal to prolong the game and win late through card advantage; it runs a higher curve and fewer creatures.
- **Midrange** blends aggro and control — efficient threats plus interaction — and is the most common Limited archetype.
- **Tempo (aggro-control)** pairs cheap instant-speed interaction with efficient creatures; it rewards careful sequencing to balance mana between threats and reactive spells.
- **Ramp** sacrifices early turns to build a superior mana position that enables high-cost threats.
- True combo decks are almost never viable in Limited; do not overvalue synergy at the expense of overall card quality.
- A cohesive synergistic deck built from commons can beat a disorganized deck holding two bombs — don't "marry" your first pick.

## Deck Construction

- The standard 40-card Limited deck is **17 lands and 23 spells**, with roughly 15–17 of the spells being creatures.
- 17 lands is mathematically sound: typical decks need their first three land drops and want their fourth, but don't need a fifth by turn five.
- Adjust land count by curve: run **16 lands** with a low/aggressive curve, **18 lands** with a high curve or a deck that can't function missing its fourth land drop, and 19+ only with an exceptionally top-heavy curve.
- A common rule of thumb: 16 lands if 3 or fewer cards cost 5+ mana, 17 lands if 4 or more cost 5+.
- If you lack enough quality spells, run an extra land rather than force a weak nonland card into the deck.
- Most decks want roughly **15–18 creatures** (about 13–18 is the workable range), with the large majority running 15 or more.
- Decks typically run 6–9 non-creature spells — usually fewer than 10.
- A common baseline framework: ~16–18 creatures, 3–4 removal spells, and 2–3 combat tricks.
- A "platonic" curve for a 17-land deck is roughly {2 one-drops, 7 two-drops, 6 three-drops, 4 four-drops, 3 five-drops, 1 six-drop}.

## Mana Curve

- Weight the curve toward cheap cards: aim for roughly 7–12 cards at 1–2 mana, with a median around 7 two-drops.
- Most cards should cost 2–3 mana, tapering off from there; lacking early plays makes you fall behind on tempo.
- Keep the top end light — about 85% of decks run 5 or fewer cards at 5+ mana; too many expensive cards gets you out-tempoed.
- You want to be able to cast a creature nearly every turn, which requires creatures spread across mana costs — missing drops loses tempo.
- An aggressive curve wants ~6–9 creatures under 3 mana, 5–10 at 3–4 mana, and only 2–3 cards above 4 mana.
- Lands are excluded from the mana curve — the curve reflects only the mana values of your spells.
- As a tiebreaker between otherwise-equal cards, take the cheaper one (the "Martin Juza rule") to keep the curve low.

## Mana Base, Fixing & Splashing

- Run **at least 8 sources of each primary color** to reliably cast your colored cards (~82% chance to have a source in your opening hand); this drives a typical 9/8 land split favoring the color with more or cheaper cards.
- Never run fewer than eight lands producing your main color.
- **Mana-fixing lands (dual lands, Evolving Wilds, etc.) count toward your land total, not as spells.** Dual lands count once toward the land total but contribute a color source to _both_ their colors simultaneously.
- A splash means adding just a card or two outside your main colors.
- A splash needs at least **three sources** of the splashed color (the "Rule of Three"); if you can't reach three sources, don't run the splash.
- Splash sources scale with splash cards: ~3 sources for one splash card, ~4 for two — using a baseline of about three lands per off-color mana symbol.
- Single-pip cards (many bombs and removal spells) are easy to splash; cards with two off-color pips are much harder.
- Situational, synergy-dependent, or curve-reliant cards make poor splash targets — they lose value when cut off from their supporting pieces.
- Splashing is safer in Sealed than in Draft, because Sealed games run slower and punish an uncastable splash card less.

## Common Mistakes

- Committing to and sticking with early picks instead of pivoting on what the table is passing is the most common draft-ruining mistake.
- Forcing a predetermined archetype or color before the draft ("forcing") — read signals and stay open instead.
- Playing too many colors and building a greedy mana base; a boring, consistent two-color deck that reliably casts its cards usually beats a greedy one.
- A fully functional deck (cards work together, good curve) beats a pile of individually more powerful cards — functional decks can 3-0 a pod while "trainwreck" piles struggle.
- Consistency (guaranteed lands and castable cards on turns 2–5) beats raw power (one bomb rare) over a large sample of games.
- Overloading on expensive spells and neglecting the early curve — you must survive the early game to cast your big spells.
- Drafting good _cards_ instead of a good _deck_; prioritize shoring up your weak slots and overall deck power over squeezing extra value from your best cards.
